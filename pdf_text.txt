Abstract—Multimodal deep learning models for cancer sur-
vival prediction frequently exhibit unstable and irreproducible
performance, limiting their clinical applicability. Rather than
proposing a new predictive architecture, this work presents a
diagnostic investigation into the sources of this instability. Using a
transformer-based deep fusion model combining RNA sequencing
and histopathology data from the TCGA-BRCA cohort, we
demonstrate that global survival prediction is highly unstable
across data splits. We then analyze the learned multimodal
embeddings in an unsupervised manner, revealing stable latent
patient subgroups with significantly divergent survival outcomes.
These subgroups are not explained by canonical receptor status
or immune infiltration, but instead align with tumor-intrinsic
proliferative activity. Conditioning survival modeling on the
largest discovered subgroup recovers strong and stable prognostic
performance, substantially outperforming unimodal baselines.
These findings indicate that survival predictability is conditional
on latent heterogeneity and suggest that heterogeneity-aware
modeling strategies are essential for robust multimodal prog-
nostication.
Index Terms—Multimodal learning, survival analysis, trans-
formers, patient heterogeneity, histopathology, genomics
I. INTRODUCTION
The estimation of a patient’s future clinical outcome, such
as survival time, disease progression, or recurrence risk, based
on biological, pathological, and clinical data is known as
cancer prognostication. Since the realisation that no single
data modality can adequately capture the complexity of tumour
biology, multimodal deep learning has emerged as a potential
paradigm. Molecular profiling is a laboratory method that uses
a sample of tissue, blood, or other body fluid to check for
certain genes, proteins, or other molecules that may be a sign
of a disease or condition, such as cancer. One of the technique
includes RNA sequencing which provides transcriptional pro-
grams, oncogenic signalling pathways, and cellular states. On
the other hand, histopathology images encode spatial organisa-
tion, morphological patterns, and microenvironmental context.
Integrating these two modalities of data should produce more
accurate survival predictions as compared to unimodal meth-
ods [1], [2].This idea was tested out on pancancer settings
and increasingly sophisticated fusion strategies [2], [3]. More
recently, transformer-based attention-based architectures have
been suggested for the construction of complex cross-modal
interactions, thereby expanding the representational capacityof multimodal models [4]. These efforts suggest that deep
multimodal fusion can uncover prognostically relevant signals
that are inaccessible to individual modalities.
The translation of multimodal survival models into reliable
clinical tools remains limited in spite of the progress. A persis-
tent and under-discussed problem is the instability of model
performance shown by substantial variance of reported con-
cordance indices across random data splits, cross-validation
folds, or minor changes in preprocessing [5]. Although models
achieve competitive average performance, they behave almost
randomly in some validation sets. This undermines the confi-
dence in their predictions [6]. This phenomenon is particularly
seen in survival analysis, where the datasets are generally
small, event rates are low, and the model outputs affect high
clinical decisions [7].
This performance instability is reduced through refinement
of architecture, hyperparameter tuning or increased regularisa-
tion, and is implicitly treated like a technical nuisance in most
of the existing studies. This perspective assumes the existence
of a single, global prognostic function for the entire patient
cohort and that the cause of instability is mainly from insuffi-
cient modelling capacity or suboptimal training procedures.
This paper explores the possibility that this assumption is
flawed for heterogeneous diseases like cancer. Instability may
be a diagnostic signal that shows the presence of unmodelled
patient heterogeneity [8].
Breast cancer is a very good example of this challenge.
It is known that breast cancer is not a single disease entity;
it is a collection of biologically distinct subtypes with di-
vergent molecular characteristics and clinical trajectories [9].
These differences are present at the receptor level or gene
expression signatures as well as in tumour morphology and
treatment response. This implies that patients within a single
cohort may follow fundamentally different survival-generating
mechanisms. A global survival model trained on such a diverse
population will learn an “average” representation. It may fit
no subgroup well, therefore, resulting in high variance in
performance across data splits.
This issue is aggravated in multimodal settings. Multimodal
fusion increases representational richness as well as model
capacity and the effective degrees of freedom. This imbalance
between model complexity and available outcome information
in event-limited survival datasets like the TCGA (The Cancer
Genome Atlas) can increase variance and overfitting [7]. But
this does not imply that multimodal models are unsuitable for
survival prediction. It suggests that their failure modes are
more subtle and closely tied to the structure of the underlying
population than is accepted.
A related issue is the evaluation of survival models. The
most common used metric for assessment of survival per-
formance is the concordance index (C-index) [10]. Though
the C-index is an important summary statistic, it collapses
model behaviour across all patients into a single scalar
value. In heterogeneous cohorts, this aggregation can con-
ceal strong subgroup-specific performance and hide systemic
failure modes. As pointed out earlier, reliance on global C-
index values may lead to deceptive conclusions about model
utility, in particular when performance varies substantially
across patient subpopulations [5]. As a result, instability in
global metrics should be used as a potential indicator of latent
structure rather than dismissed as noise.
Recent studies of machine learning in medical imaging are
focusing on the importance of understanding why models
fail [6]. These critiques argue that most of the reported
advances are weakened by inadequate evaluation practices,
hidden dataset biases and a lack of diagnostic analysis. In
the context of multimodal survival prediction, there is a need
to move beyond architecture-centric benchmarks and toward
investigations into the relationship between model behaviour
and underlying biological heterogeneity.
This study adopts a diagnostic perspective. This paper
deals with the investigation of the causes of instability in a
standard transformer-based multimodal survival model, instead
of proposing a novel fusion architecture or optimising for
maximum global C-index. The main theory is that instability
arises because survival predictability is conditional on latent
patient heterogeneity. Deep multimodal fusion can expose
this heterogeneity even when it fails to produce stable global
predictions.
To test this theory, we conduct a sequential empirical
investigation using the TCGA Breast Invasive Carcinoma
(TCGA-BRCA) cohort [11]. First, the global multimodal
survival prediction is found, and its instability is explicitly
characterised. Then, the trained model is repurposed as a
representation learning tool. Fused multimodal embeddings are
extracted, and unsupervised analysis is performed. This helps
to assess whether prognostically relevant structure emerges in
the learned interaction space or not. It should be noted that
this analysis does not use survival outcomes, receptor status
or clinical labels.
The multimodal embeddings divide patients into stable
subgroups with markedly different survival outcomes. These
subgroups are not recoverable from unimodal representations
or naive feature concatenation. This shows that deep mul-
timodal fusion captures interaction-level information that is
not visible to individual modalities. Also, the discovered
subgroups cannot be explained by canonical breast cancer
markers such as receptor status. Instead, it can be alignedwith tumour-intrinsic biological characteristics, particularly
proliferative activity [9].
Survival prediction becomes accurate and stable when con-
ditioned on a homogeneous patient subgroup. Here, multi-
modal modelling will substantially outperform unimodal base-
lines. It is consistent with histopathology and molecular data
that provide complementary prognostic information [1], [3].
Across resampling, conditional modelling exhibits minimal
variance. Negative control experiments confirm that instead
of statistical artefacts, these gains reflect genuine prognostic
signal [6].
Together, these findings support a reframing of multimodal
survival modelling. Instead of asking whether optimising
global state-of-the-art performance on heterogeneous cohorts
is sufficient [6], the more fundamental question is how
multimodal representations encode latent biological structure.
This structure conditions survival predictability [8], [9]. By
shifting the focus from aggregate performance metrics toward
diagnostic analysis [5], this work highlights the importance
of understanding when and for whom multimodal survival
prediction is feasible. This also motivates heterogeneity-aware
modelling strategies as a path toward more robust and clini-
cally meaningful prognostication.
II. RELATEDWORK
A. Multimodal Survival Prediction
An active area of research is multimodal survival prediction.
It has been motivated by the hypothesis that integrating het-
erogeneous biomedical data sources can yield more accurate
and biologically grounded prognostic models than unimodal
approaches. Early work by Mobadersany et al. [1] and subse-
quent pan-cancer analysis by Kather et al. [12] showed that
combining histopathology images with genomic features using
convolutional neural networks improved outcome prediction
compared to either modality alone. This study provided one
of the first large-scale demonstrations that complementary
prognostic information is contained in molecular profiles and
histological morphology.
Subsequent work extended multimodal survival modelling
to pancancer settings. Cheerla and Gevaert [2] proposed a deep
learning framework integrating genomics, clinical variables,
and imaging-derived features across multiple cancer types.
Their results showed improved average concordance indices
compared to unimodal baselines. This reinforces the potential
of multimodal learning. Vale-Silva and Rohr [3] also explored
multimodal architectures for long-term cancer survival predic-
tion. They showed the benefits of feature fusion in deep neural
networks.
In spite of these promising results, most multimodal survival
studies focus on improving global predictive performance.
Model evaluation is done using mean C index values across
cross-validation folds, with limited discussion of performance
variance or subgroup-specific behaviour [6]. Therefore, the ro-
bustness and interpretability of these models in heterogeneous
clinical populations remain open questions. Furthermore, re-
liance on global concordance indices can be misleading, as
these metrics may be biased by the censoring distribution and
fail to capture time-dependent performance variations [5].
B. Deep Learning Models for Survival Analysis
Along with multimodal research, numerous work was done
on developing deep learning methods for survival analysis.
DeepSurv [13] introduced a neural network formulation of
the Cox proportional hazards model. The Cox proportional
hazards model is a widely used semi-parametric regression
technique in survival analysis that estimates the relationship
between the time until an event occurs such as death or failure,
and one or more explanatory variables (covariates). It models
the hazard rate, assuming that the ratio of risks between groups
remains constant over time. Its main assumptions are that the
hazard ratios are constant over time, the subjects are indepen-
dent of one another, the relationship between covariates and
the log hazard is linear and that the censoring mechanism
is independent of the risk of the event. DeepSurv, on the
other hand, enables nonlinear risk modelling while preserving
the interpretability of hazard-based survival analysis. DeepHit
[14] proposed a discrete-time formulation. It is capable of
modelling competing risks, thus representing a departure from
traditional Cox-based approaches.
Studies of deep learning for survival analysis highlight
the diversity of proposed architectures. It includes recurrent
networks, multitask learning, and attention-based models [7].
These methods differ in formulation and optimisation objec-
tives. But they all want to improve global performance metrics.
Importantly, few studies interrogate the stability of these
metrics across heterogeneous patient populations or explore
whether performance variability reflects underlying biological
structure [6], [8].
C. Transformer-Based Fusion
In medical applications for multimodal learning, attention
mechanisms and transformer architectures have become very
popular. Through self-attention and cross-attention mecha-
nisms, transformers are a flexible framework for modelling
interactions within and between modalities. Multi-modal sur-
vival models leverage these capabilities to capture conditional
relationships between histopathology, genomics, and other
data sources.
The PS3 framework [4] is an example. It integrates
pathology reports, histology images, and biological pathways
using a multimodal transformer. PS3 demonstrates improved
survival prediction performance across multiple cancer cohorts
by modelling interactions between modalities. Evaluation pri-
marily centres on global performance metrics, leaving open
questions about how learned representations behave in hetero-
geneous populations, just like earlier approaches.
D. Image-Based Genomic Signal Discovery
Several studies have shown that deep learning models can
extract molecular information directly from histopathology
images. Kather et al. [12] showed that convolutional neural
networks trained on WSIs (Whole Slide Images) could predictclinically actionable genetic alterations across multiple cancer
types. These provide strong evidence that morphological pat-
terns encode latent molecular signals.
Multimodal frameworks like MOMA [15] also empha-
sise explainability and cross-cohort validation in integrating
pathology images with multi-omics data. These studies focus
on classification or molecular inference rather than survival
modelling. They also establish preceden for deep learning
models uncovering non-trivial biological structure through
multimodal integration.
E. Patient Heterogeneity and Evaluation Pitfalls
Biological heterogeneity is a defining feature of cancer.
Marusyk and Polyak [8] expressed the evolutionary and mi-
croenvironmental origins of tumour heterogeneity. They high-
lighted its implications for treatment resistance and clinical
outcomes. Large-scale molecular analyses, like those con-
ducted by Hoadley et al. [9], also demonstrate that tumours
cluster into biologically distinct subtypes across tissues of
origin.
From the context of machine learning, heterogeneity
presents a challenge for global predictive modelling. Unsuper-
vised clustering has been used to identify latent structure in
high-dimensional biological data for a long time, but clustering
stability and interpretability remain critical concerns. V on
Luxburg [16], [17] provides a theoretical foundation for
clustering methods and stability assessment. She shows the im-
portance of robustness to initialisation and sampling variabil-
ity. Analogous challenges arise in single-cell transcriptomics,
where clustering results can vary depending on algorithmic
choices and preprocessing [18]. These observations need
careful validation when interpreting unsupervised subgroup
discovery, particularly in clinical contexts.
F . Evaluation Pitfalls and Diagnostic Perspectives
The limitations of aggregate performance metrics in het-
erogeneous datasets are becoming popular. The concordance
index can hide subgroup-specific behaviour and inflate confi-
dence in unstable models [5], [10]. Recent studies of medi-
cal AI emphasise that model evaluation must move beyond
headline metrics to include diagnostic analyses that probe
failure modes and dataset biases [6]. In this context, few
multimodal survival studies investigate why models fail or how
instability relates to underlying biological structure [7]. This
gap motivates the present work, which positions instability as
a signal pointing to latent heterogeneity.
G. Positioning of the Present Study
This study adopts a diagnostic framework to understand
the relationship between multimodal fusion, heterogeneity,
and survival predictability. It does not propose a new sur-
vival architecture against an exhaustive set of models. By
analysing learned multimodal embeddings and conditioning
survival modelling on discovered subgroups, this work bridges
multimodal representation learning, unsupervised subgroup
discovery, and survival analysis in a unified framework.

III. METHODS
A. Study Design and Overall Methodological Framework
This study follows a sequential diagnostic methodol-
ogy rather than a conventional performance-driven modeling
pipeline. Instead of iteratively optimising architectures to
maximise global survival prediction accuracy, we explicitly
investigate the causes of performance instability in multimodal
survival models. The methodological design therefore pro-
ceeds in three stages:
(1) construction and evaluation of a standard multimodal
survival model, (2) unsupervised analysis of learned mul-
timodal representations to identify latent patient structure,
and (3) conditional survival modeling within discovered sub-
groups.
This design choice is motivated by growing recognition
that aggregate performance metrics may obscure systematicfailure modes in heterogeneous clinical datasets [5], [6]. By
structuring the analysis sequentially, we aim to distinguish
between architectural limitations and population-level effects,
and to assess whether instability reflects latent biological
heterogeneity rather than noise or overfitting.
A high-level overview of the methodological pipeline is
illustrated in Fig. 1.
B. Dataset and Cohort Definition
All analyses were conducted on data from **The Cancer
Genome Atlas Breast Invasive Carcinoma (TCGA-BRCA)**
cohort [11]. TCGA provides paired molecular and histopathol-
ogy data generated using standardized protocols, making it
a widely used benchmark for multimodal cancer modeling
studies.
The final study cohort consisted of **193 patients** for
whom both RNA sequencing data and digitized whole-slide

histopathology images were available. Survival outcomes were
defined using overall survival time and censoring indicators
provided by TCGA clinical annotations. Within this cohort,
**29 death events** were observed, placing the study firmly
in an **event-limited survival analysis regime**.
This low event rate has important methodological im-
plications. In survival analysis, effective model capacity is
constrained not by sample size alone but by the number of
observed events [10]. This consideration directly informed
downstream design choices, including image token sampling,
model complexity, and evaluation strategy.
C. RNA-Seq Data Processing and Representation
RNA sequencing data were represented using **pre-
computed low-dimensional embeddings**, obtained via prin-
cipal component analysis (PCA) applied to normalized gene
expression profiles. Each patient was represented by a **128-
dimensional RNA embedding**, capturing dominant transcrip-
tional variation while reducing dimensionality and noise.
To ensure numerical stability during training, all RNA
embeddings were preprocessed as follows:
* Any NaN or infinite values were replaced with zero.
* Feature values were clipped to a fixed range to limit the
influence of outliers.
This preprocessing strategy mirrors common practices in
deep survival modeling and avoids introducing additional
assumptions through gene-level feature selection or pathway
aggregation. Importantly, no survival outcomes or subgroup
labels were used at this stage.D. Histopathology Image Processing and Tokenization
Digitized whole-slide images (WSIs) were processed into
patch-level feature representations using a pretrained con-
volutional neural network. Each WSI was tiled into tissue-
containing patches following foreground segmentation, and
each patch was encoded as a **2048-dimensional feature
vector**.
This patch-level representation preserves local morphologi-
cal information while enabling efficient downstream modeling.
Feature extraction was performed independently of survival
outcomes and molecular data to avoid information leakage.
For each patient, a fixed number of **(K = 8)** image
tokens were selected by **random sampling** from the avail-
able tissue patches. Patients with fewer than eight valid patches
were zero-padded to maintain a consistent sequence length
across the cohort.
The choice of (K = 8) reflects a **conservative tradeoff**
between capturing spatial heterogeneity and limiting model
capacity in an event-limited survival setting. While larger
values of (K) may encode finer-grained morphological detail,
increasing the number of image tokens also increases the
effective degrees of freedom of the model, amplifying variance
and overfitting risk given only 29 observed events.
Random sampling was intentionally adopted to avoid in-
troducing selection bias or survival-informed patch filtering.
By accepting noisy and potentially uninformative patches,
this strategy allows us to test whether meaningful structure
can emerge from minimal assumptions, strengthening the
diagnostic nature of the analysis.
All image tokens underwent the following preprocessing
steps:
* Replacement of NaN and infinite values with zero, * L2
normalisation of feature vectors, * Feature clipping to a fixed
range to stabilise training.
E. Multimodal Survival Transformer Architecture
The core model used in this study is a **transformer-
based multimodal survival network**, designed to integrate
histopathology and RNA-seq data through **deep cross-
attention**. The architecture is intentionally kept simple and
standard, as the goal is not architectural novelty but diagnostic
insight.

Following self-attention, the token sequence is aggregated
into a single fixed-length image representation using a learn-
able linear projection and mean pooling. This aggregation step
produces a holistic summary of the patient’s histopathology
while retaining information learned through attention.
Deep fusion is achieved using a **cross-attention mecha-
nism**, where the aggregated image representation acts as the
query and the RNA embedding serves as the key and value.
This design forces the model to learn **conditional interac-
tions**, effectively asking how molecular features should be
interpreted in the context of observed morphology.
This interaction space is central to the study. Prior work
has shown that simple concatenation is insufficient to model
such conditional relationships [1], [2]. Cross-attention provides
a principled mechanism for learning these dependencies and
is hypothesized to be responsible for revealing latent patient
structure.
The output of the cross-attention layer is passed through a
linear risk prediction head, producing a single scalar risk score
per patient. Higher risk scores correspond to higher predicted
hazard.
F . Training Objective and Optimization
The model was trained using the **Cox partial likelihood
loss**, a standard objective for survival analysis that ac-
commodates censored data [10]. To partially compensate for
the imbalance between censored and event samples, observed
events were upweighted by a factor of (α= 2.0).
This weighting scheme was selected to modestly emphasize
informative events without dominating the loss or destabilizingtraining. No time discretization or competing-risk modeling
was employed, as the focus of this study is representation
learning rather than survival model comparison.
G. Global Model Evaluation Protocol
Global model performance was evaluated using a **strat-
ified shuffle-split cross-validation** scheme. Each split pre-
served the event-to-censoring ratio to the extent possible. For
each fold, the model was trained from scratch and evaluated
on a held-out validation set.
Predictive performance was quantified using the **concor-
dance index (C-index)**, the most commonly reported metric
in survival modeling [10]. Rather than focusing solely on
the mean C-index, we explicitly analyzed the distribution of
performance values across splits to assess stability.
As emphasized in prior work, the C-index can obscure
heterogeneous model behavior and exhibit high variance in
mixed populations [5]. In this study, variability in global
performance is treated not as random noise but as a signal
motivating further investigation.
H. Unsupervised Discovery of Latent Patient Subgroups
After training, patient-level multimodal embeddings were
extracted from the output of the cross-attention layer.
These embeddings represent the learned interaction between
histopathology and RNA-seq features and serve as the basis
for unsupervised analysis.
Unsupervised clustering was performed using **KMeans
with (K = 2)**. Survival outcomes, receptor status, and clinical
variables were not used during clustering. The choice of (K =
2) reflects the goal of identifying coarse, prognostically distinct
subgroups rather than fine-grained subtype taxonomies.
A conceptual illustration of the clustering process is shown
in **Fig. 3**.
To assess robustness, clustering was repeated across **50
random initializations**, and agreement was quantified using
the **Adjusted Rand Index (ARI)** [17]. High ARI values in-
dicate stable partitions insensitive to initialization, addressing
known concerns regarding unsupervised clustering reliability
[18].
I. Ablation Analysis for Fusion Necessity
To determine whether discovered subgroups were specific
to deep multimodal fusion, we conducted ablation experiments
using:
1. RNA embeddings alone, 2. Aggregated image embed-
dings alone, 3. Naive concatenation of RNA and image fea-
tures.
Each representation was clustered using the same procedure,
and resulting partitions were compared to those obtained from
deep fusion using ARI. This analysis tests whether subgroup
structure is an emergent property of cross-modal interaction
rather than unimodal signal.

J. Conditional Survival Modeling
Following subgroup discovery, the multimodal survival
model was retrained using data exclusively from the **largest
discovered subgroup**. This conditional modeling approach
tests whether survival predictability improves within a more
homogeneous population.
Performance was evaluated using **bootstrap resampling
(200 iterations)** to estimate the distribution of C-index
values and corresponding confidence intervals. Results were
benchmarked against a unimodal RNA-only Cox model trained
on the same subgroup.
K. Negative Control Experiment
To validate that improved performance reflected genuine
prognostic signal rather than overfitting, a negative control
experiment was performed. Survival event labels were ran-
domly permuted, and the conditional model was retrained and
evaluated using the same protocol. Performance collapsing to
chance levels provides evidence that the original signal was
non-random.
L. Summary of Methodological Contributions
In summary, the proposed methodology reframes multi-
modal survival modeling as a diagnostic process. By integrat-
ing standard survival modeling, unsupervised representation
analysis, and conditional evaluation, this framework explicitly
connects performance instability to latent heterogeneity and
provides a principled path toward heterogeneity-aware prog-
nostic modeling.figures/global_cindex_instability.pdf

IV. RESULTS
The results are presented to mirror the sequential diagnostic
framework outlined in the Methods section. Rather than opti-
mizing for a single performance metric, we first demonstrate
the instability of global multimodal survival prediction, then
uncover its underlying cause through unsupervised analysis
of learned multimodal representations, and finally show that
conditioning survival modeling on discovered latent subgroups
restores stable and accurate prognostic performance.
A. Global Multimodal Survival Prediction Is Highly Unstable
We begin by evaluating the transformer-based multimodal
survival model on the full TCGA-BRCA cohort using strati-
fied shuffle-split cross-validation. Predictive performance was
quantified using the concordance index (C-index) for each
validation split.
As shown in **Fig. 4**, the model exhibits **extreme
variability** in performance across splits. While some folds
achieve moderately high C-index values, others approach or
fall below random performance. The resulting distribution
spans a wide range, despite identical architecture, hyperpa-
rameters, and training protocol.
The mean C-index alone is therefore insufficient to char-
acterize model behavior. Instead, the **variance** of per-
formance highlights a fundamental lack of robustness. Im-
portantly, this instability persists despite the use of deep
multimodal fusion, suggesting that architectural expressiveness
alone does not resolve the issue.

B. Unsupervised Clustering of Multimodal Embeddings Re-
veals Latent Structure
To investigate the source of instability, we analyze the
internal representations learned by the model. Specifically, we
extract patient-level multimodal embeddings from the output
of the cross-attention fusion layer and perform unsupervised

I. Summary of Key Findings
Taken together, the results demonstrate that:
1. Global multimodal survival prediction is highly unstable
in heterogeneous cohorts, 2. Deep multimodal fusion learns
stable latent representations that encode prognostic structure,
3. This structure is biologically meaningful and aligned with
tumor aggressiveness, 4. Conditioning survival modeling on
discovered subgroups restores stability and accuracy.
These findings collectively support the central claim of
this work: survival predictability is conditional on latent het-
erogeneity**, and instability in global models reflects this
underlying structure rather than architectural failure.
V. DISCUSSION
This study set out to investigate a phenomenon that is widely
observed but rarely interrogated in multimodal cancer survival
modeling: the instability of global predictive performance.
Rather than proposing a new architecture or optimizing for a
higher average concordance index, we adopted a diagnostic
perspective to understand why multimodal survival models
frequently fail to generalize reliably, even within a single, well-
curated cohort. The results provide compelling evidence that
such instability is not merely a technical artifact, but a direct
consequence of unmodeled latent patient heterogeneity.
A. Rethinking Instability in Multimodal Survival Models
A central finding of this work is that global multimodal
survival prediction on the TCGA-BRCA cohort is highly un-
stable across data splits. While this observation might initially
be interpreted as evidence of model inadequacy, our analysis
suggests a different interpretation. The instability arises despite
the use of a transformer-based architecture with sufficient
representational capacity to model complex cross-modal in-
teractions. This indicates that the failure is not architectural in
nature.
Instead, the instability reflects a deeper issue: the assump-
tion that a single prognostic function can adequately model
survival across a biologically heterogeneous population. In dis-
eases such as breast cancer, where tumors differ substantially
in molecular drivers, growth dynamics, and microenvironmen-
tal interactions, this assumption is unlikely to hold. When a
global model is trained on such a mixed population, small
changes in the composition of training and validation splits
can dramatically alter the effective prognostic function being
learned, leading to high variance in performance.
From this perspective, instability is not noise to be sup-
pressed, but a **signal** indicating the presence of multiple,
competing survival-generating mechanisms within the cohort.
Recognizing instability as informative rather than incidental
represents a conceptual shift in how multimodal survival
models should be evaluated and interpreted.
B. Deep Multimodal Fusion as a Tool for Heterogeneity
Discovery
One of the most striking results of this study is that, despite
failing to produce stable global predictions, the multimodal
transformer learns patient representations that encode robust
and prognostically meaningful structure. Unsupervised clus-
tering of the fused embeddings reveals stable latent subgroups
with markedly different survival outcomes, even though sur-
vival labels were not used during clustering.
This finding highlights a crucial distinction between **pre-
dictive performance** and **representational quality**. A
model may fail as a global predictor while still learning a
highly informative representation of the data. In our case,
the cross-attention mechanism enables the model to learn
conditional relationships between histopathology and gene
expression, producing an interaction space in which latent
heterogeneity becomes visible.
Importantly, this structure does not emerge from unimodal
representations or naive feature concatenation. The ablation
results demonstrate that neither RNA-seq data nor histopathol-
ogy alone contains sufficient information to recover the discov-
ered subgroups. Instead, the prognostically relevant structure isan emergent property of deep multimodal fusion. This supports
the view that the value of multimodal learning lies not only
in aggregating information, but in how different biological
modalities condition and contextualize one another.
C. Biological Interpretation of Latent Subgroups
A critical question in any unsupervised analysis is whether
discovered structure corresponds to meaningful biology or
merely reflects technical artifacts. Several lines of evidence
support the biological validity of the latent subgroups identi-
fied in this study.
First, the clusters are not explained by canonical breast
cancer receptor status. The absence of significant enrichment
for ER, PR, or HER2-defined subtypes indicates that the model
is not simply rediscovering known clinical categories. This
negative result is important, as it rules out a trivial explanation
for the observed survival separation.
Second, the clusters exhibit clear differences in proliferative
activity, as evidenced by differential expression of MKI67.
Increased proliferation is a well-established marker of aggres-
sive tumor behavior and is consistent with the poorer survival
observed in the high-risk cluster. This alignment between
molecular biology and clinical outcomes provides a coherent
biological interpretation of the latent structure.
In contrast, immune-related differences between clusters are
modest, suggesting that immune infiltration is not the primary
driver of the discovered heterogeneity. Together, these findings
indicate that the multimodal model captures tumor-intrinsic
aggressiveness rather than superficial clinical or immune fea-
tures. This supports the hypothesis that deep multimodal fusion
can uncover subtle, interaction-level biological signals that are
not accessible through unimodal analysis.
D. Conditional Survival Predictability and Its Implications
Perhaps the most consequential result of this study is the
dramatic improvement in survival prediction achieved through
conditional modeling. When the multimodal survival model is
retrained exclusively within the largest discovered subgroup,
predictive performance becomes both high and remarkably sta-
ble. This contrasts sharply with the unstable behavior observed
in the global setting.
This result demonstrates that **survival predictability is
conditional** on population homogeneity. In other words, the
difficulty of survival prediction is not uniform across patients;
some subgroups admit a well-defined prognostic function,
while others do not. Global modeling obscures this distinction
by forcing a single function to fit all patients simultaneously.
The comparison with an RNA-only baseline further under-
scores the value of multimodality in this conditional setting.
Histopathology contributes meaningful prognostic information
beyond gene expression alone, but only when the population
is sufficiently homogeneous. This finding helps reconcile con-
flicting reports in the literature regarding the utility of mul-
timodal fusion: its benefits may be highly context-dependent
and masked in heterogeneous global analyses.
E. Methodological Implications for Multimodal Survival Re-
search
The results of this study have several important method-
ological implications for future research.
First, they challenge the prevailing emphasis on global
performance metrics. Reporting a single mean C-index without
examining variance or subgroup behavior can be misleading,
particularly in heterogeneous cohorts. Diagnostic analyses that
probe stability and representation structure should become a
standard component of multimodal survival modeling.
Second, the findings suggest that multimodal models should
be evaluated not only as predictors, but also as represen-
tation learning tools. Even when predictive performance is
unstable, learned embeddings may encode valuable biological
information that can inform subgroup discovery, hypothesis
generation, and downstream modeling.
Third, the success of conditional modeling points toward
**heterogeneity-aware pipelines** as a promising direction.
Rather than training a single global model, future approaches
may benefit from a two-stage strategy: first, use multimodal
fusion to identify latent subgroups; second, train simpler and
more stable prognostic models within each subgroup. Such
an approach aligns more closely with the biological reality of
cancer as a collection of related but distinct diseases.
VI. CONCLUSION
Multimodal deep learning has long been proposed as a path-
way toward more accurate and biologically informed cancer
prognostication. However, despite increasingly sophisticated
fusion architectures, multimodal survival models continue to
exhibit unstable and irreproducible performance, limiting their
clinical utility. In this work, we presented a diagnostic inves-
tigation into the origins of this instability, reframing it not as
a technical deficiency but as a symptom of unmodeled latent
patient heterogeneity.
Using a transformer-based multimodal survival model ap-
plied to the TCGA-BRCA cohort, we demonstrated that global
survival prediction is highly unstable across data splits, even
when modern deep fusion mechanisms are employed. Rather
than attempting to optimize away this instability, weanalysedd
thelearntd multimodal representations and showed that deep
cross-attention uncovers stable latent patient subgroups with
markedly different survival outcomes. These subgroups were
discovered in a fully unsupervised manner and were shown to
be robust, biologically coherent, and not trivially explained by
known clinical subtypes.
Crucially, we found that survival predictability is conditional
on this latent heterogeneity. When survival modeling was
restricted to a homogeneous subgroup, predictive performance
became both stable and substantially improved relative to
unimodal baselines. This result demonstrates that the appar-
ent failure of global multimodal survival models conceals
meaningful prognostic structure that can be unlocked through
heterogeneity-aware analysis. Moreover, the negative control
experiments confirmed that the observed improvements reflectgenuine biological signal rather than overfitting or information
leakage.
A. Limitations
Despite these findings, several limitations must be acknowl-
edged. First, the analysis was conducted on a cohort with a
limited number of observed survival events, which constrains
the statistical power of global modeling and may inflate per-
formance estimates in conditional settings. Although bootstrap
evaluation and label-shuffling controls were employed, larger
cohorts with higher event rates are required to confirm the
robustness of the results.
Second, all experiments were performed on a single retro-
spective dataset derived from TCGA. While TCGA provides
standardized multimodal data and serves as a valuable bench-
mark, external validation on independent cohorts is necessary
to establish generalizability and clinical relevance.
Third, this study focused exclusively on RNA sequenc-
ing and histopathology images. Other relevant modali-
ties—including somatic mutations, copy number alterations,
epigenetic profiles, treatment information, and longitudinal
clinical variables—were not considered and may further in-
fluence latent heterogeneity and survival predictability.
Finally, the choice to identify a small number of coarse-
grained subgroups reflects a deliberate tradeoff between inter-
pretability and granularity. More fine-grained subgroup discov-
ery may reveal additional structure but would require careful
methodological safeguards to avoid overfitting and spurious
findings.
B. Future Work
The results of this study point toward several promising
directions for future research. A key next step is the application
of this diagnostic framework to larger and more diverse
multimodal cohorts, including external validation datasets,
to assess the reproducibility of the discovered heterogeneity
across populations and clinical settings.
Methodologically, future work could explore adaptive or
hierarchical subgroup discovery strategies that balance inter-
pretability with biological resolution. Integrating additional
data modalities, such as genomic alterations, epigenetic mark-
ers, and treatment histories, may further refine subgroup
definitions and enhance prognostic insight.
From a modeling perspective, the findings motivate the de-
velopment of heterogeneity-aware survival pipelines, in which
multimodal models are explicitly used to identify latent pa-
tient structure prior to conditional prognostic modeling. Such
pipelines may yield more stable and clinically meaningful
predictions than monolithic global models.
Finally, beyond survival prediction, the learned multimodal
representations themselves may serve as a basis for down-
stream biological discovery, including the identification of
novel tumor phenotypes or treatment-responsive subgroups.
Leveraging multimodal deep learning as a diagnostic and
exploratory tool, rather than solely as a predictive engine, rep-
resents a promising paradigm for future work in computational
oncology.
